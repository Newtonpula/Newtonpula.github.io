Synapses

The previous chaper introduces two classes of ion channels, namely voltage-activated and calcium-activated ion channels. The third type of ion channel here are the transmitter-activated ion channels involved in synaptic transmission and generally activated from outside the cell.

  • release of neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft.
  • transmitter molecules diffuse to the other side of the cleft.
  • ionotroic receptors located in the postsynaptic membrane are activated, influencing on the state of an associated ion channel.
  • Metabotropic receptors control the state of an ion channel by means of a biochemical cascade of G proteins and second-messengers.

In both cases, the activation of the receptor results in excitatory or inhibitory postsynaptic transmembrane current (EPSC or IPSC)

Inhibitory Synapses

GABA(gamma-aminobutyric acid): Important neurotransmitter at inhibitory synapses

Channel subtypes GABA-A and GABA-B

  • GABA-A: ionotropic and open exclusively for chloride ions
  • GABA-B: have metabotropic receptors that trigger a comparatively slow signaling signaling chain ultimately leading to the opening of $K^{+}$ channels

Excitatory Synapses

Glutamate: Important neurotransmitter at excitatory synapses.

  • AMPA channel: rapid, calcium cannot pass if open
  • NMDA(N-methyl-D-aspartate) channel: slow, calcium can pass if open

In the subthreshold regime, excitatory synapses always depolarize the membrane, i.e, shift the membrane potential to more positive values.

Rapid Synaptic Dynamics

We concentrate on dyamic changes of the synapse that do not persist but decay back to their normal values within hundreds of milliseconds or a few seconds. Thes changes are called short-term synaptic plasticity.

Spatial Structure: Axons

In terms of propagation dynamics, we distinguish two types of axons: the myelinated and the unmyelinated axons. Myelin is useful to increase propagation speed in far-reaching projections.

Unmyelinated Axons

Unmyelinated axons contain sodium and potassium channels uniformly distributed over their entire length. The general equation for ion channels imbedded on a passive membrane is

For the giant squid axon, the ionic current are described by the Hodgkin-Huxley model

In other systems, the axon may be covered with other types of sodium or potassium ion channels.

The action potential propagates by activating sodium channels along the cable rather than by spreading the charges such as in a passive dendrite. The properties of the ion channels strongly influence conduction velocity.

Two spikes traveling in opposite direction will collide and annihilate each other.

When the impedance mismatch at the point where a single axon splits into two can is significant, the action potential can reflect and start travelling in the direction it came from.

Myelinated Axons

Myelinated axons have sodium and potassium channels only in restricted segments called nodes of Ranvier. These nodes form only 0.2% of the axonal length, the rest is considered a passive membrane that is wrapped into a myelin sheath.

Myelin mainly decrease the membrane capacitance $C$ and increase the resistance $R_{T}$ by a factor of up to 300.

Assuming that the nodes are equally separated by a myelinated segment of length $L$, we can model the evolution of the membrane potential at each node $u_n$. The dynamics of idealized myelinated axons follow Kirchoff’s equation with a resistance $R_{L}=Lr_{L}$ replacing the myelinated segment

where $C$ is the total capacitance of the node.

The conduction velocity is greatly increased by myelin such that some nerves reach 70-80 m/s.
